Title:

Advances in wearable sensors and interfaces: Cyber-physical systems for bio-signals real-time processing and actuators control


Presenter:

Daniela De Venuto, Politecnico di Bari, Italy


Description:

Recent advancements both in wireless communication and integrated circuits design enabled low-power, smart, miniaturized, invasive/non-invasive sensor nodes, in, over or around the human body and usable in various applications, including health monitoring. The Wireless Body Area Networks (WBANs) aims to simplify, speeding up, and improve the accuracy of the interaction between sensors and actuators making real the so-called Internet of Thing (IoT) or everything.
Since it has been proven that most diseases can be prevented if precociously detected, the importance of the WBANs is going to grow in the context of early diagnosis as for instance in cardiovascular, Parkinson’s, obesity, diabetes and other chronic diseases. The combination of concepts as the sensors network, the IoT (Swarms) and the computational capability of the wearable devices open the door for true human augmentation.
Yet, the bottleneck is making sense of the plethora of information received from the often noisy sensors and making reliable decisions within very tight latency bounds (< 10 ms) without huge computational workloads to be performed in wearable form factors at extreme energy efficiency. The purpose of this tutorial is to follow the overall workflow of a WBAN-based analytic system. Starting from the description of the electronic sensor interface, the signal wireless transmission, and the reading systems that all together, constitute the body area network, the lecture will go up to the wireless connection to devices able to real-time process the sensors data. Particular attention will be given to the brain (EEG) and muscular signals (EMG), as well as to the hardware architectures (e.g., FPGA, μProcessor) for the real time sensor data processing. A last step will be dedicated to the possibility of the automatic control of mechatronic actuators based on user-centered features.
The course proposes the state of the art on bio-signals based applications, which exploit different embedded cyber-physical systems (CPS) implemented on FPGA. The CPSs interface non-invasive wearable sensors, analyze the gait and postural instability in Parkinson’s disease, provide cognitive diagnosis and remotely drive a mechatronic device (e.g., prototype car).


About the speaker:

Prof. Dr. Daniela De Venuto is professor of Electronics at Politecnico di Bari, Italy, where is also scientific director of the Design of Electronic Integrated System Laboratory (DEISLab). Her main scientific interests are on smart detectors and sensors, focusing on their readout circuits and on these topics, she leads several national and European projects and collaborations. She is member of the directive committee of the Embedded Systems Laboratory inside the National CINI Lab. She initiated and is Chair of the IEEE Advances in Sensors and Interfaces Workshop (IWASI), which successfully continues over years since 2005, in collaboration with IEEE CPMT Society. Since 2010 she is IEEE ISQED Fellow.
She has been visiting professor in several outstanding Universities and Research Centers in Europe and US. She has been working, during a sabbatical stay at NXPs in Leuven, Belgium and at HTC of Eindhoven (NL). Currently, she is collaborating with the EECS dept. of University of California at Berkeley (US) and is visiting professor at Berkeley Wireless Research Center (BWRC). She is consultant for several companies/laboratories (e.g., Exprivia, Sitael, INNOVAALAB). Prof. De Venuto owns one international patent on digital testing strategy for high-resolution sigma-delta AD converters and is author of around 200 papers on international Journal and on Conference proceedings. She got several best paper awards at several international conventions. She is member of several technical committee or European representative for several IEEE Conferences, and editor and guest editor of numerous IEEE Journal (IEEE Sensor Journal, IEEE Trans. On CAS, IEEE Tran. On CAD…)
